Overview

mLearn is a cross‑platform desktop application for Super-Fast Language Learning through immersion. Watch your favorite content in your target language—even as a complete beginner—using native media.

More information is available on [a href="https://mlearn.morisinc.net"]mlearn.morisinc.net[/a].

The app is fully open source; you can find its source code [a href="https://github.com/adrianvla/mlearn"]here[/a].

Features

  • Live word translation
  • Knows what words you know, and adjusts itself based on that
  • Seamless integration with Anki
  • Advanced Flashcard Creation
  • Known word blurring
  • Power‑efficient and offline mode
  • Supports Japanese & German
  • Streams any type of video
  • Very customizable
  • Support for Japanese pitch accent
  • Support for custom languages
  • Word frequency list (e.g., JLPT)
  • Custom SRS system with automatic flashcard creation
  • Language exam study mode (e.g., JLPT-oriented vocabulary selection) to help you ace your exam.
  • Advanced Exam vocabulary knowledge statistics
  • Access to an internal database to edit word definitions

Supported languages

For now, Japanese and German.

Why I built this

I began developing mLearn in Summer/Autumn 2024 after starting Japanese in February 2024. Native media were inaccessible at a beginner level.

I needed an app that enabled immersion with any content—including offline sources—with Anki integration. I tried [a href="https://migaku.com/"]Migaku[/a], but it was not free and did not support my goals for native‑level content.

That motivated me to build a complete language learning app focused on immersion, even for beginners.

Because I could not understand most Japanese at the time, I created a live word translator that appends translations based on readings.

It helped significantly: the first series I watched using mLearn was [a href="https://www.imdb.com/title/tt2098220/"]Hunter x Hunter[/a] with Japanese subtitles and the original dub.

mLearn helped me reach conversational fluency. I learned over 1,000 words with minimal friction, as I later discovered in an internal database.
